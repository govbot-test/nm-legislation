1 A MEMORIAL
3 ON THE STATUS OF WOMEN TO STUDY AND DEVELOP A STATEWIDE PLAN
4 OF ACTION TO ADDRESS THE FORMER GOVERNMENT POLICY OF FORCED
5 OR COERCED STERILIZATION OF INDIGENOUS WOMEN AND OTHER WOMEN
6 OF COLOR IN NEW MEXICO AND TO LAY THE GROUNDWORK FOR A STATE
7 TRUTH AND RECONCILIATION COMMISSION, A MEMORIAL TO VICTIMS OF
8 FORCED OR COERCED STERILIZATION AND STATE ACKNOWLEDGMENT OF
9 THE INHUMANITY OF THE GRIEVOUS POLICY.
10
11 WHEREAS, between 1907 and 2018, Indigenous women and
12 other women of color across the United States, including in
13 New Mexico, were subjected to forced or coerced
14 sterilizations without their free, prior and informed
15 consent, often through the federal bureau of Indian affairs
16 and its contracted health care or the bureau's successor, the
17 Indian health service and its direct services and
18 purchased-referred care, which includes physicians and other
19 health care providers, private practices, clinics and
20 institutions; and
21 WHEREAS, investigations in the 1970s revealed that
22 between twenty-five and fifty percent of Indigenous women of
23 childbearing age had been sterilized, with some of the
24 highest concentrations of procedures occurring in the Indian
25 health service regions of Albuquerque and the broader SM 14
Page 1
1 southwest; and
2 WHEREAS, federal and independent inquiries, including
3 those conducted by the United States government
4 accountability office in 1976 and Indigenous researchers,
5 such as Dr. Connie Pinkerman-Uri (Choctaw/Cherokee), have
6 documented widespread violations of medical ethics and human
7 rights, including sterilizations performed on girls and women
8 under duress or without being given full information about
9 the sterilization procedure and its consequences, or even
10 without any permission being sought; and
11 WHEREAS, these acts constitute grave violations of
12 fundamental human rights, including the rights to health,
13 bodily autonomy, family and freedom from torture or cruel,
14 inhuman or degrading treatment, as recognized under the
15 Universal Declaration of Human Rights and the Convention on
16 the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women
adopted by the United Nations general assembly and the Rome
17
Statute of the International Criminal Court, which classifies
18
forced sterilization as a crime against humanity; and
19
WHEREAS, the reproductive violence perpetrated against
20
Indigenous women through forced or coerced sterilization has
21
caused historical and generational trauma, demographic loss
22
and cultural devastation, harms that many legal scholars and
23
human rights experts argue rise to the level of genocide; and
24
WHEREAS, New Mexico, as a state with a majority-minority
25 SM 14
Page 2
1 population and a deep legacy of Indigenous leadership, bears
2 a moral and historical responsibility to confront this
3 unacknowledged chapter of United States and state history;
4 and
5 WHEREAS, transitional justice frameworks, including
6 truth commissions established in Canada, South Africa and the
7 state of Maine, demonstrate that processes of truth-telling,
8 reparative justice and institutional reform can support
9 healing communities and restore public trust in government;
10 and
11 WHEREAS, the establishment of a New Mexico memorial to
12 the victims of forced or coerced sterilization and the
13 creation of a truth and reconciliation commission would make
14 New Mexico the first state in the nation to formally
15 investigate, acknowledge and redress the full scope of these
16 violations; and
WHEREAS, Indigenous women leaders, advocates and
17
survivors, alongside scholars and health practitioners, are
18
organizing to develop a Native American-led framework for
19
culturally grounded reproductive justice and trauma-informed
20
care to ensure such violations never occur again; and
21
WHEREAS, in 2025, during the United Nations
22
permanent forum on the rights of Indigenous peoples, the
23
American Indian movement-west delegation met with
24
Dr. Albert K. Barume, the United Nations special rapporteur
25 SM 14
Page 3
1 on the rights of Indigenous peoples, to raise the issue of
2 forced sterilization of Native American women and to call for
3 a United Nations global study on the scale and scope of these
4 crimes; and
5 WHEREAS, Dr. Barume has since agreed to undertake a
6 visit to the United States, including a commitment to
7 visit New Mexico, to meet with communities and survivors,
8 engage with state and tribal officials and hold an
9 international press conference on this issue, thereby
10 positioning New Mexico as a focal point for global truth and
11 healing;
12 NOW, THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED BY THE SENATE OF THE
13 STATE OF NEW MEXICO that the Indian affairs department and
14 the commission on the status of women be requested to conduct
15 a comprehensive study into the history, scope and continuing
16 impacts of forced and coerced sterilization of Indigenous
women and other women of color in New Mexico; and
17
BE IT FURTHER RESOLVED that the study:
18
A. identify all known and potential cases of
19
forced or coerced sterilization conducted within New Mexico
20
through United States Indian health service facilities and
21
contracted physicians and other hospitals and clinics;
22
B. gather survivor testimony using trauma-informed
23
and culturally safe protocols, in partnership with
24
Indigenous-focused and Indigenous-community-led
25 SM 14
Page 4
1 organizations;
2 C. assess the availability and accessibility of
3 reproductive health services for Indigenous women and other
4 women of color in New Mexico today; and
5 D. review and recommend educational, policy and
6 reparative measures to prevent future violations and promote
7 community healing; and
8 BE IT FURTHER RESOLVED that the department and the
9 commission be requested to seek the advice and assistance of
10 victims and family members of victims of forced or coerced
11 sterilization, historians, social and health services
12 practitioners and others knowledgeable about the treatment by
13 government employees and agents against Indigenous women and
14 other women of color and trauma-informed and culturally
15 sensitive protocols to be used when discussing such treatment
16 with victims and their families; and
BE IT FURTHER RESOLVED that upon completion of the
17
study, the department and commission be requested to present
18
their findings and recommendations to the governor and the
19
legislature no later than December 31, 2027, including
20
proposals for:
21
A. the establishment of a New Mexico truth and
22
reconciliation commission on the forced or coerced
23
sterilization of Indigenous women and other women of color;
24
B. the creation of a statewide Native American-led
25 SM 14
Page 5
1 reproductive justice and reproductive sovereignty program
2 focused on culturally grounded health, education and healing
3 services;
4 C. the development of a public memorial and
5 educational curriculum to ensure enduring remembrance and
6 education of this history; and
7 D. the acknowledgment by the state of the
8 inhumanity of the grievous policy of forced or coerced
9 sterilization of Indigenous women and other women of color;
10 and
11 BE IT FURTHER RESOLVED that copies of this memorial be
12 transmitted to the secretary of Indian affairs, the chair of
13 the commission on the status of women, the governor, tribal
14 governments in New Mexico, the interim legislative health and
15 human services committee and the interim legislative
16 committee dealing with Indian affairs. SM 14
